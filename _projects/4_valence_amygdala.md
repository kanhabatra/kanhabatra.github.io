A central function of the amygdala is to assign **emotional valence** — to tag stimuli and
experiences as positive (rewarding) or negative (aversive) — so that the organism can learn
appropriate approach and avoidance behaviors. How the brain implements this valence assignment
at the circuit level is a long-standing question.

This project, led by Hao Li and Praneeth Namburi in the **Tye Lab**, investigated the role of
**neurotensin (NT)**, a neuropeptide richly expressed in limbic circuits, in orchestrating valence
assignment in the **basolateral amygdala (BLA)**. My contribution focused on the computational
analysis of large-scale neural population recordings — characterizing how ensemble dynamics in
the BLA shift in response to NT, and how distinct cell populations tile the valence axis.

Using dimensionality reduction and population decoding approaches, we demonstrated that NT
selectively modulates BLA circuits to enable the flexible assignment of positive or negative
valence to neutral stimuli, with distinct cell types serving opponent roles in encoding
appetitive versus aversive associations.
